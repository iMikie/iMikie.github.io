No SQL?

Even a new student of WebTech can't help but notice the rise of "noSQL" databases. What are they and do I need to learn them? A quick glance over to Wikipedia reveals an abysmal article full of jargon and little explanation. I imagine some gnome whose only purpose was to demostrate that he knows something I don't. Even over at Mongo you get painfully awkward sentences like "Some key-value stores, such as Redis, allow each value to have a type, such as "integer", which adds functionality". Take away: integers add functionality to some things. Seriously, maybe I should get a job as a writer instead.

OK, so what can we say? It appears that noSQL has become popular with the rise of social media and the massive data generated from spying on every aspect of users online lives: what they purchase, the web-pages they visit, how long they stay there, who they are connected to, etc. If SQL was designed to connect customers with products and record their orders, this new type of data needs something different. OK, so what choices do we have?

NoSQL Database Types

  • Document databases pair a search key with data structured into a "document". Documents can contain many different key-value pairs, or key-array pairs, or even nested documents.
  • Graph stores are used to store information about networks, such as social connections. It looks like these are nodes containing data with links between nodes.
  • Wide-column stores such as Cassandra and HBase are optimized for queries over large datasets, and store columns of data together, instead of rows.
  • Graph appear to be implemented as serialized objects that have pointers to other objects. The graph database manager can follow these networks of objects.

So why use a no-SQL database? Oh, 'cause they are more scalable and offer superior performance. Note to reader: if you are ever asked to be a marketing person, just write "more scalable and better performance". It doesn't really matter what you are selling. I ran into that phrase a lot. OK, here's what we can say...maybe.

Storing Data when you don't know it will be

Relational databases require that schemas be defined before you can add data. For example, you might want to store data about your customers such as phone numbers, first and last name, address, city and state. So if you decide, a few iterations into development, that you'd like to store customers' favorite items in addition to their addresses and phone numbers, you'll need to add that column to the database, and then migrate the entire database to the new schema.

Distributing the database

Here is another "gotta have" feature which is never explained well. If your data grows without bounds, then eventually it might be nice to divide it over several servers. It's easy to see that if you want to search for customers with in some location then if that data is on several servers, you gotta talk to all the servers. Evidentially there is no easy way to split up Relational tables and still query against the entire dataset. Evidentially as well there are ways to provide this with noSQL databases automatically. Alas, I was not able to find a competent explanation as to how this is done. Just accept that it is.

Replication and Redundancy

Backing up an SQL database and keeping both copies up-to-date is evidentially difficult but easy to do with a non SQL database. Once again, this claim was repeated over and over, while I could not find a simple explanation. Unfortunately I can't tell you why and how this works.

It's pretty clear that the particular suitability of a given NoSQL database depends on the problem it must solve but unfortunately the tech writers these companies hire don't seem to be able to give any concrete examples. In the meantime I'm sure it's "more better", less downtime, more agile, non-proprietary...
